# Phases

## Phase 1: Damaged Ship or Ship-Like Habitat Survival

### Starting State

- The player begins inside a damaged ship or ship-like habitat with tight interior space.
- The opening uses a finite-reserve model:
  - Finite oxygen reserve
  - Finite water reserve
  - Ration-based food
  - Limited power access
- The player is living off reserves first, not generating sustainable systems immediately.
- No fully sustainable life support exists at start.
- The player is not inheriting a mostly functional weak base.
- Broken infrastructure is expected, including examples like disconnected vents and broken pipe routes.

### Opening Burst

- The first minutes should be short and legible.
- There should be one obvious immediate problem that demands action.
- The player should reach a quick first stabilisation, then pivot into branching priorities.
- Multiple viable follow-up paths should exist immediately after the first stabilisation.
- The opening should set tone strongly without becoming a long mandatory repair checklist on every run.

### Repair and Extension Pattern

- Reserves are consumed while the player restores critical routes and functionality.
- Repairing or reconnecting one system should create pressure on another.
- Player-built stopgaps should extend survival but not grant permanent stability.
- Different extension paths should be viable so the player is making choices rather than following a single correct solution.
- Representative pattern, not locked final implementation:
  - Start with oxygen reserve in a tank and damaged distribution routes.
  - Restore oxygen distribution first through repair and reconnect work.
  - Add oxygen generation later, which consumes scarce water and power while adding heat pressure.

### Pressure Hierarchy

- Current direction is that oxygen is the most legible immediate driver.
- Water and power gate how long survival can be extended.
- Heat is present in Phase 1 and becomes the defining environmental identity later.
- This hierarchy is directional and tuning-dependent, not final locked numbers.
- This hierarchy is intended to guide tuning, not restrict alternate viable player strategies.

### Structural and Content Notes

- Outer hull and key vessel-identity elements can be fixed to preserve scenario identity.
- Interior structure should likely use a hybrid fixed/destructible model.
- Fully non-destructible interiors are likely too restrictive for gameplay.
- Layout should constrain the player without preventing necessary system placement or routing.
- Blocked sections, visible supplies behind barriers, and gated access are useful when they do not choke the critical path.
- Some content can be gated by research or stronger digging capability, but core progression should stay readable and fast.

### Optional Content Direction

- One optional sealed hazard room is a strong current direction.
- Reward should be meaningful enough to justify risk.
- Hazard examples include heat, vacuum, harmful gas, leaks, or radiation.
- Opening this room should be worth considering but never immediately mandatory.
- Only a small number of such rooms should exist to avoid clutter and maintain clarity.

### Replayability Constraints

- The intro should remain short, strong, and replayable.
- Repair topology, access order, resource placement, or optional pressure composition should vary enough to prevent rote play.
- Critical path communication should remain clear so replay variation does not become confusion.

## Transition: Descent Readiness and Commit

### Hard Capability Gate and Soft Readiness Layer

- Hard capability gate: minimum restoration state required to make descent possible at all.
- Soft readiness layer: preparation depth that determines whether descent is survivable.
- The hard gate should feel like restoring ship capability, not unlocking a gameplay checkpoint.

### Design Goals

- Once descent is unlocked, the player should face a real judgment call on timing.
- The player should feel pressure to move on, but not from a single hard timer alone.
- Descent readiness should feel like ship restoration and mission triage, not an arbitrary checklist.

## Mid Game: Hostile Beachhead

### Initial State

- Descent leads to a harsh beachhead phase, not a normal base transition.
- Initial foothold is intentionally limited and pressured.
- Initial foothold should feel risky and inefficient rather than immediately scalable.
- Planet conditions are actively hostile from the start.

### Gameplay Focus

- Establish minimal survivable foothold systems first.
- Keep the ship relevant as fallback, staging, and support for a while.
- Expand cautiously under pressure rather than normalizing quickly into comfort flow.

### Success Criteria

- Beachhead survives repeated environmental stress.
- Ship-to-foothold relationship remains strategically meaningful during early foothold development.

## Late Game: Sustained Hostile-World Operation

### Identity

- The world should never become fully safe.
- The player engineers around hostility and contains it, but does not erase it.
- Player systems should scale to manage pressure, but never eliminate it entirely.
- End-state target is managed industrial success under ongoing hostility, not comfort-state colony success.

### Primary Hazard Direction

- Heat is the first concrete implementation path.
- Difficulty should come from persistent world pressure rather than changing how vanilla machines behave.

### Win Direction

- Directional endpoint is sustained production, industrial accomplishment, or system-level hostile-world mastery.
- This is not standard escape endgame progression.
- Final implementation details remain intentionally open.
